Phylogenetic analysis Genomic DNA was extracted from fresh 3-day-old YPD cultures using the thermolysis method (Zhang et al. ). The D1/D2 domain of LSU rRNA was amplified using primers N1/N4, while the ITS region was amplified with primers ITS1/ITS4. PCR conditions followed Yan et al. (). PCR products were sequenced by ShengGong Bioscience (Beijing, China). Phylogenetic analyses were performed following Yan et al. (). Combined analysis of D1/D2 and ITS sequences revealed that isolates CGMCC 2.8612 and CGMCC 2.8613 clustered within Coniochaeta , forming a distinct clade (MLBP = 100, BIPP = 1.00) phylogenetically distinct from other congeneric species. These isolates showed a close affinity to Coniochaeta marina type strain MFLUCC 18-0408 (MLBP = 92, BIPP = 0.99; Figure S2). The same analysis clustered isolates CGMCC 2.8608 and CGMCC 2.8609 clustered within Wickerhamomyces , forming a distinct lineage (MLBP = 100, BIPP = 1.00) separate from other taxa in this genus (Figure S3). This clade showed strong affiliation with a subclade containing W. anomalus type strain NRRL Y-3668 (MLBP = 98, BIPP = 1.00; Figure S3). Similarly, isolates CGMCC 2.8610 and CGMCC 2.8611 were placed within Filobasidium , forming a clade with four F. uniguttulatum strains (Figure S4). 2.4. Antifungal susceptibility Antifungal susceptibility testing was performed against four clinically used antifungals: fluconazole, amphotericin B, terbinafine, and itraconazole. The assay followed the CLSI M38 broth microdilution method with minor modifications. Coniochaeta diabeticopedicola CGMCC 2.8612, F. uniguttulatum CGMCC 2.8610, W. paranomalus CGMCC 2.8608, and W. paranomalus CGMCC 2.8609 were cultured on 20% YPD agar at 37 °C for 2 days under static conditions. Conidia were harvested using 1 mL sterile 0.85% saline, vortexed briefly, and adjusted to an optical density (OD530) of 0.13. The suspension was diluted 1:50 in RPMI 1640 broth, and 100 μL aliquots were added to antifungal-containing wells. After 72 h incubation at 37 °C, absorbance was measured at 530 nm in triplicate. The in vitro antifungal susceptibility testing results of four prepentive isolates are shown in Table S1. Both C. diabeticopedicola CGMCC 2.8612 and W. paranomalus CGMCC 2.8609 are sensitive to fluconazole and amphotericin B, the corresponding minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) fluconazole and amphotericin B are 8 μg/mL, 1 μg/mL, and &lt; 0.03125 μg/mL and 0.0625 μg/mL, respectively. C. diabeticopedicola CGMCC 2.8612 is also sensitive to itraconazole. However, Filobasidium uniguttulatum CGMCC 2.8610 and W. paranomalus CGMCC 2.8608 are resistant to fluconazole, amphotericin, and terbinafine. 3. Discussion The genus Coniochaeta (Coniochaetaceae, Ascomycota) is typified by Coniochaeta ligniaria (Grev.) Massee and comprises pleomorphic fungi with yeast-like asexual morphs. As of 9 Sep 2025, Index Fungorum recognizes 130 validly published Coniochaeta species. Phylogenetic analyses of LSU and ITS sequences revealed that our isolates form a distinct lineage within Coniochaeta , and the type strain CGMCC 2.8612 presents only 93.74% (D1/D2) and 92.43% (ITS) sequence identity to the ex-type strain of C. marina . Additional divergence in colony morphology and thermotolerance (growth at 37 °C) supports the introduction of a new species, Coniochaeta diabeticopedicola sp. nov. Generally, Coniochaeta spp. exhibit a broad ecological distribution, inhabiting soil, water, decomposing organic matter, and food substrates (Si et al. ). Among these species, C. hoffmannii and C. mutabilis are well-documented human pathogens (Shinohara et al. ), while C. massiliensis was recently described from a clinical specimen in France (Kabtani et al. ). Our findings demonstrate that C. diabeticopedicola represents a novel clinical species isolated from a diabetic foot ulcer. This highlights the expanding spectrum of environmental fungi with pathogenic potential, emphasizing their increasing threat to human health (Brown et al. ). Like other dimorphic fungi, C. diabeticopedicola displays a yeast-like growth phase (), a virulence-associated trait. Unlike previously reported Coniochaeta pathogens, the type strain exhibited no resistance to conventional antifungal agents; however, the severity of infection necessitated amputation. The genus Wickerhamomyces (type species: W. canadensis ) was segregated from Pichia based on phylogenetic and phenotypic criteria (Kurtzman ). Currently comprising 42 species, these yeasts are distinguished by narrow-based budding and variable hyphal formation. In spite of our isolates grouped well with Wickerhamomyces anomalus , the type strain CGMCC 2.8608 shares 99.5% (586/589 bp, D1/D2) and 98.3% (507/516 bp, ITS) identity with W. anomalus ex-type strain NRRL Y-3668, yet their thermotolerance at 37 °C and less length/width ratio of conidia suggest a novel taxon, Wickerhamomyces paranomalus sp. nov. Although Wickerhamomyces spp. are primarily industrial microorganisms (producing biosurfactants and enzymes), opportunistic infections by W. anomalus , W. myanmarensis , and W. onychis have been reported (Denham et al. ). Here, we introduce W . paranomalus as a novel human pathogen associated with dermatomycosis CGMCC 2.8608 and seborrheic dermatitis CGMCC 2.8609. Because W. paranomalus CGMCC 2.8608 is resistant to the testing antibiotics, clotrimazole was applied to treat the female patient with skin scraping on the neck, and the patient was cured after externally for 3 weeks. Wickerhamomyces paranomalus CGMCC 2.8609 is sensitive to all testing antibiotics, however, the male patient who was suffering from severe seborrheic dermatitis was treated with selenium sulfide lotion topically and was cured after 4 weeks. The different resistance to antibiotics between CGMCC 2.8608 and CGMCC 2.8609 will be further investigated for understanding the mechanism of drug resistance of Wickerhamomyces . Filobasidium uniguttulatum , first isolated from human nails, is a rare but known agent of keratitis and meningitis. In this study, it was recovered from a case of tinea capitis (Figures S3,S4), further confirming its clinical relevance. Despite resistance to tested antibiotics in vitro (CGMCC 2.8610), the infection resolved following 6-week topical terbinafine therapy. In conclusion, through comprehensive phylogenetic analyses employing multilocus sequence data (D1D2, ITS) and detailed phenotypic characterization, two novel species, Coniochaeta diabeticopedicola sp. nov. and Wickerhamomyces paranomalus sp. nov., and one known fungal pathogen, Filobasidium uniguttulatum were identified. Those findings expand our knowledge of fungal biodiversity in cutaneous infections and underscore the importance of species-level identification in clinical mycology to optimize patient management strategies.
